Riley's Manual
A guide to how I process, for the people who matter
I feel everything. I think out loud. I show love by showing up. I've been dyslexic my whole life, and for a long time that just meant "bad at school." It took me a while to see that my brain isn't broken, it just runs different software. I built this because the people closest to me deserve to understand what's actually happening when I do the things that confuse them. And because I wanted to see it clearly myself. This is what I know so far.

Some of my patterns
"I read the room before I read the words. Tone, posture, and eye contact tell me everything. Sometimes more than the person intended."

I pick up on shifts in energy that most people miss. A slight change in someone's breathing, a jaw tightening, a pause that's half a second too long. My body registers it before my conscious mind does. This makes me a good friend. It also makes me exhausting to live with because I'm reacting to signals the other person doesn't know they're sending.

With Quinn, I can feel when they're approaching overload before they know it themselves. The problem is that I respond to the signal I'm reading instead of what they're actually telling me. I'm solving for what I sense, not what they've said.

↑ Trust that I'm picking up something real, even if you can't name it yet. "I don't know what's happening but you might be right" goes a long way.
↓ "I'm fine, you're imagining things." I'm not imagining it. My body already registered the shift. Denying it makes me doubt my own perception.

Jan 25 — At dinner with friends, I could feel Quinn starting to fade 30 minutes before they said anything. Their shoulders tightened, their responses got shorter. I leaned over and said "we can leave whenever." They looked relieved. Later said they didn't know I'd noticed.
"When someone goes quiet, I fill the silence. It's not pressure. It's panic."

Silence feels dangerous to me. Not rationally. Somewhere in my body. When Quinn goes still, my brain immediately starts running: what did I do? Are they upset? Did I miss something? The fastest way to resolve that feeling is to talk. Ask questions. Fill the space. Get a signal back.

I know now that Quinn's silence isn't about me. But knowing it doesn't stop my body from reacting like it is. I'm working on sitting in the quiet. It's one of the hardest things I do.

The other side of this: nobody in Quinn's life monitors their wellbeing as closely as I do. The same attentiveness that makes me fill silence is what makes me the first person to notice when something is actually wrong. My radar is always on. That's a cost for me and a gift for the people I love.

↑ A small signal before going quiet. "I need a minute, it's not about you." That one sentence saves me hours of spinning.
↓ Going silent without any signal and then being frustrated that I'm asking what's wrong.

"I process by talking. I don't know what I think until I hear myself say it."

Writing is hard for me. Not the ideas. The mechanics. Typing takes twice as long, I second-guess every word, and by the time I've written a paragraph I've lost the thread I started with. But put me in a conversation and the ideas flow. I think out loud. I circle, I contradict myself, I land somewhere I didn't expect.

This can be a lot for Quinn. They need to process internally first. I need to process externally. When we're both trying to figure something out at the same time, I'm talking and they're shutting down and we're both frustrated because we're using opposite systems.

But when someone gives me the space to think out loud, I land on ideas that surprise both of us. Some of the best decisions Quinn and I have made came from me rambling for 10 minutes and then suddenly saying the thing that changed everything. The mess is the method.

↑ Let me ramble without holding me to every word. I'm finding my way to the point, not making my final argument.
↓ "You just said the opposite of what you said two minutes ago." I know. That's how the process works.

Quinn communicates beautifully in writing. Precise, clear, exactly the right words. I can't do that. But put me in a room and I'll say something that makes you feel understood in a way no email ever could. My words land differently when they're spoken. That's where my communication lives.

↑ Call me. Or let me voice-note you. I'll say more in 30 seconds of talking than I can write in 30 minutes.
↓ Long text messages that expect a long text back. I'll either not respond or send something that sounds cold because I ran out of bandwidth mid-sentence.

What energizes me
"Put me in front of a problem that needs creative thinking and I'll run for hours. My brain doesn't do step-by-step. It does leaps."

I see the whole shape before I see the pieces. Where most people work through a problem linearly, my brain throws connections across the room. This looks scattered from the outside. From the inside it's the most alive I feel. Give me a whiteboard, give me a person to bounce off of, and I'll find an angle nobody else considered.

The catch is I can't always explain how I got there. The logic is real but the path isn't sequential. Quinn asks "how did you get to that conclusion?" and my honest answer is "I don't know, but I know it's right." That drives them crazy.

What drains me
"Reading long documents empties my battery faster than anything. It's not the content. It's the format."

The words swim. I re-read the same paragraph three times and retain nothing. By page two my brain is spending more energy on decoding than understanding. Give me the same information as a conversation or a video and I'll absorb it instantly. The knowledge is accessible. The delivery format isn't.

Quinn sends beautifully written messages. I love that about them. But when it's three paragraphs, my brain shuts down halfway through and I respond to the first thing I remember, which is usually not the most important thing they said.

Tell me the same thing in person and I'll remember details a week later that you forgot you said. My memory for spoken information is exceptional. The knowledge gets in. It just needs a different door.

↑ Lead with the most important thing. Or call me. Or say "the short version is..." before the long version.
↓ "Did you even read what I wrote?" Probably not all of it. Not because I don't care. Because the format defeated me before the content could land.

In meetings when someone turns to me and says "Riley, what do you think?" my brain goes blank. Not because I don't have thoughts. Because the pressure to produce language on demand activates the same shame response I've had since childhood. Suddenly I'm 10 years old being asked to read aloud in class. The content is there. The performance pressure locks it up.

Give me a heads up. "Riley, I'll ask you about the Q3 numbers in 10 minutes." That's enough. The same brain that froze on demand will deliver something insightful with 10 minutes of prep. I'm not lacking the knowledge. I'm lacking the runway.

↑ Warn me before asking me to speak. Even 60 seconds. Send me the agenda beforehand.
↓ Cold-calling me in a group. The shame response wipes out everything I actually know.

How I show care
"I show up. Physically. Consistently. That's my language. Watch what I do, not what I type."

I'll drive an hour in traffic to bring you soup when you're sick. I'll sit with you in a waiting room for three hours and not look at my phone. I'll cancel plans to be where you need me. None of that will show up in a text message. My written words are short and clumsy. My actions are where the love lives.

Quinn writes the most beautiful messages. I can't compete with that on paper. But I've never missed a moment when they needed me in the room. I need them to know those two things aren't in competition. They're two different languages.
