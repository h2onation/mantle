Quinn's Manual
A guide to how I process, for the people who matter
I built this because the people I care about deserve more than guessing. I process the world differently and for most of my life I couldn't explain how. Now I can. I was diagnosed autistic at 31, and a lot of things suddenly made sense. This is what I've figured out so far about how I operate, what I need, and what I'm giving you even when it doesn't look the way you expect.

Some of my patterns
"I'm direct because I value honesty. When I say 'that won't work,' I mean exactly that. No subtext."

I'm sharing information as accurately as I can. It genuinely doesn't occur to me that stating a fact could be received as an attack. People at work call me blunt. My partner's family thinks I'm cold. I've been told to "soften" my delivery but I don't know what that means in practice. Adding words that aren't true feels dishonest.

The flip side: people who know me trust what I say. If I say something is good, it's good. There's no guessing game.

↑ Take what I say at face value. Ask me to elaborate instead of assuming intent.
↓ "You don't have to be so harsh." Now I'm confused and ashamed, and less likely to share next time.

"When plans change without warning, I freeze. It looks like resistance. It's actually recalculation."

My brain builds a map for what's going to happen. When the plan changes, the map gets erased and I need a few minutes to build a new one. That's all that's happening. But in those few minutes I go still, and my partner reads that as inflexible. "Why can't you just go with the flow?" Because the flow requires a map and you just erased mine.

↑ Even 10 minutes warning. "Plans might change." That's enough to loosen the map before it breaks.
↓ "It's not a big deal" or "just relax." Both dismiss what's actually happening, and the freeze deepens.

"When multiple people talk at once, I can't filter. I hear everything at equal volume. I'm not ignoring you. I'm drowning."

Every voice, every background noise, every clink of a glass arrives at the same priority. I can't select what to listen to. So I go quiet at dinner. I leave parties early. My partner thinks I don't enjoy their friends. I do. I enjoy them one at a time, in a quiet room. Not six at once over music.

The same sensitivity that drowns me in groups makes me extraordinary in the right setting. In a quiet conversation I catch things nobody else hears. Shifts in tone, hesitations, the word someone almost said. My hearing isn't broken. It's unfiltered. That's a cost in a crowd and a gift in a conversation.

↑ Smaller groups. Quieter venues. Permission to step outside for 10 minutes without it being a thing.
↓ "You're being antisocial." Adds social pressure on top of sensory overload.

What drains me
"After a full day of masking, I'm running on empty. I'm not withdrawing from you. I'm recovering from everyone else."

Masking is performing neurotypicality all day. Eye contact on demand, laughing at the right times, monitoring my face, my tone, my posture. It's exhausting at a level that's hard to explain to someone who does all of that automatically.

I come home and go silent. My partner has said "you're fun with other people but shut down with me." It's the opposite of what it looks like. They are the person I can stop performing with. That's the highest compliment I know how to give.

↑ Quiet together. Both doing our own thing in the same room. No expectation of conversation. Just presence.
↓ "What's wrong?" when nothing is wrong. "Did I do something?" when the answer is no but I can't be convincing about it.

When someone says "how's it going?" I have to decide: do they want a real answer or the script? How long should my answer be? What's my face doing? Am I making enough eye contact? Most people run this software in the background. I run it manually, every time.

But skip the small talk and ask me something real, and I'll give you the most honest, undistracted conversation you've had all week. The same brain that can't do surface-level is built for depth.

Mar 1 — Office day. Counted 23 separate "how's it going" interactions by lunch. Each one required a real-time decision about what to say, how to say it, what face to make. By 2pm I couldn't form sentences in the team meeting.
"Ambiguity exhausts me faster than any workload."

If my partner says "it would be nice to clean up a bit this weekend," I'm stuck. Does that mean right now? Saturday morning? Is it a request or a suggestion? What counts as "a bit"? My brain treats it as an open loop that runs in the background consuming energy until it's closed.

"Can you vacuum the living room Saturday morning?" takes me two seconds. "It would be nice to clean up a bit" takes me all day.

The flip side of needing precision is that I deliver it too. When I commit to something, you know exactly what you're getting. No vague promises. No "I'll try." If I say I'll do it, I mean the specific thing at the specific time. My need for clarity makes me one of the most reliable people you know.

↑ Specific requests. Time, place, scope. The more precise, the easier it is for me.
↓ Open-ended suggestions that sound optional but aren't. My brain can't resolve the ambiguity.

How I show care
"I research solutions to your problems because that's how my brain says I love you."

When my partner mentioned their shoulder was hurting, I spent two hours reading about rotator cuff exercises, found a physiotherapist with good reviews near their office, and texted them a summary. I'm learning that sometimes the most caring thing I can do is not solve. But I want them to know that when I do solve, it's not detachment. It's the deepest attention I know how to give.
